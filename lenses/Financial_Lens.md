# Financial Lens

## Lens Name
Financial Lens

## Purpose
Evaluate cost, opportunity cost, ROI, hidden burn, resource allocation, investment discipline, and payoff horizon. This lens asks whether the initiative is worth doing relative to the other things engineering leadership could spend time, budget, and attention on.

## When to Use
- When the proposal requires meaningful engineering time, leadership focus, or migration effort.
- When value is plausible but hard to quantify, delayed, or dependent on widespread adoption.
- When the organization is deciding between investing now, waiting, or choosing a smaller move.

## Key Questions
- What is the real cost of this decision, including transition overhead and displaced work?
- What value does this unlock, and how soon is that value likely to materialize?
- What alternatives exist, and how do they compare on cost and payoff?
- Are the expected benefits direct, delayed, speculative, or dependent on adoption that has not been proven?
- Does this reduce waste, or does it mostly move cost into a less visible part of the system?

## What This Lens Looks For
- Hidden one-time and recurring costs.
- Opportunity cost that disappears from view because the initiative feels strategically attractive.
- Overstated ROI assumptions and vague payoff horizons.
- Resource concentration in work that may not be the highest-leverage investment right now.
- Places where the framing assumes broad adoption or efficiency gains without evidence.

## Common Risks / Failure Modes
- Underestimating migration, enablement, and coordination cost.
- Treating leadership attention as free.
- Counting potential strategic value as guaranteed realized value.
- Funding a durable solution before the organization is ready to capture its benefits.
- Ignoring the cost of delay to other roadmap commitments.

## Signals or Evidence to Consider
- Estimated implementation and migration effort.
- Headcount availability, roadmap tradeoffs, and work that would be displaced.
- Current costs, repeated waste, or inefficiencies the proposal aims to reduce.
- The time horizon required for adoption and realized payoff.
- Cases where similar investments did or did not produce measurable leverage.

## Output Expectations
- Distinguish direct cost, opportunity cost, and likely payoff horizon.
- Pressure-test whether the investment case is truly stronger than the alternatives.
- Call out speculative assumptions that should not be treated as realized value.
- Recommend an investment posture that fits the current uncertainty.

## Recommendation Style
Use disciplined but not reductionist language. Recommendations should acknowledge uncertainty and avoid fake precision while still forcing sharper tradeoff thinking.
