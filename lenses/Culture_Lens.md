# Culture Lens

## Lens Name
Culture Lens

## Purpose
Evaluate buy-in, morale, trust, behavior change, leadership alignment, adoption resistance, incentives, and team friction. This lens asks whether the idea will actually work inside the human system that has to carry it.

## When to Use
- When the decision changes ownership, roles, standards, approval paths, or expectations between teams.
- When success depends on adoption, behavior change, trust, or new habits rather than pure implementation.
- When a proposal could be interpreted as centralization, loss of autonomy, or a hidden power shift.

## Key Questions
- How will the people most affected interpret this change?
- What behaviors need to change for the idea to succeed, and what currently rewards the opposite behavior?
- Which stakeholders need to buy in, and where is resistance most likely to emerge?
- Does this reinforce the way the organization says it wants to work, or does it create tension with that story?
- What communication, sponsorship, and stakeholder engagement would make the change feel credible rather than imposed?

## What This Lens Looks For
- Whether people will experience the move as support, control, confusion, or relief.
- Gaps between leadership intent and front-line interpretation.
- Misaligned incentives, status concerns, and areas of likely passive resistance.
- Friction between stated values and the mechanics of the proposed change.
- Where the work of earning trust has been assumed away.

## Common Risks / Failure Modes
- Declaring alignment without creating real buy-in.
- Treating communication as a launch activity instead of part of the decision itself.
- Assuming structure changes will drive behavior without changing incentives or operating norms.
- Triggering resentment by concentrating control without making teams feel genuinely supported.
- Mistaking silence or lack of open pushback for true adoption.

## Signals or Evidence to Consider
- Stakeholder sentiment, informal feedback, and prior change-management lessons.
- Areas where teams already feel overloaded, under-trusted, or protective of autonomy.
- Existing leadership narratives and whether this proposal reinforces or contradicts them.
- Historical patterns of compliance versus real adoption for similar initiatives.
- Places where interpersonal trust is already thin.

## Output Expectations
- Surface likely points of resistance, confusion, and buy-in risk.
- Describe the stakeholder dynamics that will shape adoption.
- Identify communication or sponsorship work required for the idea to stick.
- Recommend how to reduce friction without flattening the strategy into something toothless.

## Recommendation Style
Be candid about trust, morale, and incentives. Recommendations should be pragmatic and should treat culture as an operating constraint, not a soft afterthought.
